Feeding Your Pigs

Home Feeding Your Pigs

Pigs require consistent feeding based on their age, sex, growth stage, and other factors. Sows and boars may be fed once or twice a day, while growing pigs benefit from 24-hour access to feed. Note that pigs cannot live on pasture alone and must not be fed meat or kitchen scraps due to health and legal concerns.

Types of Feeds

Bought from feed mills, designed to meet full nutritional needs. Best growth, higher upfront cost, simplest to manage. It is important to note that pigs cannot be raised on pasture alone and will require additional feed for proper growth and development. There are several things you need to consider when feeding your pigs. It is also important to know that it is illegal to feed any meat or meat products to your pigs. This is one reason not to feed table scraps. Complete Feeds Complete feeds are typically purchased from a feed mill and designed to meet all the nutritional requirements of your pigs. Different feeds will be required for different stages of growth, and your feed company can assist you with this. This might seem like the most expensive approach upfront, but it is also the simplest, and your pigs will have the best growth.

Can include grains like wheat, barley, peas, and lentils. Requires vitamin/mineral supplements. Cheaper, but slower growth. Some people will prefer to make (grind) their own feed to save on cost. These diets can consist of a single grain (e.g. wheat or barley) or multiple grains (e.g. wheat/barley/peas/ lentils) with or without by-products (distillers, screenings). These diets will require an additional vitamin/mineral supplement to ensure nutritional requirements are met. Diets tend to be cheaper than complete feeds; however, pigs will also tend to grow more slowly. Vitamin/mineral supplements can be found at your local feed mill. Produce-Based Homemade Feeds In some cases, produce (fruits and vegetables) may provide a low-cost opportunity in feeding your pigs. These diets will require a very specific vitamin/mineral supplement to ensure nutritional needs of the pig are met. While it is acceptable to use produce in a feeding program, you must ensure it has never entered a kitchen or has been in contact with meat or products of animal origin. Typically, supermarket or market garden leftovers would provide a viable option for sourcing these types of products. DO NOT feed food/table scraps as they can be contaminated with meat products. Feeding any product that contains meat or meat products is illegal in Canada. Nutrition

Use clean, non-contaminated fruits and vegetables. Must never have touched meat or kitchen environments.

Nutrition Components

  • Energy: From wheat, barley, corn (for growth and maintenance)
  • Protein: From soybean meal, peas, lentils. Avoid overfeeding due to cost.
  • Vitamins & Minerals: Use complete feed or supplements to meet needs.

Alternative Ingredients

Approved ingredients include bakery waste, distillers grains, and cereal residue. Do not use meat products.

Feed Quantity & Method

Adjust feed based on pig needs and environmental factors. Overfeeding breeding pigs can harm performance. Ensure efficient feeding with proper feeders and reduce waste.

Feed Safety Tips

  • Avoid moldy feed
  • Clean spills quickly
  • Use secure, dry storage
  • Avoid contaminated hay/straw

Toxic Plants & Risks

  • Mycotoxins: Growth issues, sickness, reproductive issues
  • Redroot Pigweed: Weakness, death
  • Cocklebur: Vomiting, weakness, death
  • Black Nightshade: Anorexia, death

Feeders

  • One 15-inch space can serve up to 18 pigs
  • Use weatherproof, pest-proof feeders
  • Ensure all pigs can access feed
  • Use hoppers for large numbers of pigs
